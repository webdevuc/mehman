Etihad Airways

Etihad Airways P.J.S.C. is the national airline of Abu Dhabi. Etihad Airlines, one of the fastest-growing airlines in aviation history, stunned the industry in 2004 when it placed an $8 billion order for aircraft just one year after its founding. A few luxury airlines had already established themselves in the neighboring emirates of Abu Dhabi. Still, Etihad's royal backers hoped it would be able to carve out its niche in the rapidly changing global aviation landscape. Fueled by oil revenue, the airline aimed to boost tourism and commerce while contributing to Abu Dhabi's economic diversification. Every year, the airline transports over four million passengers and a large amount of freight.

History

Established in July 2003, the first scheduled flight of Etihad Airways P.J.S.C. took place on November 12 of that year. The first fleet consisted of six leased planes.

One of the United Arab Emirates, Abu Dhabi, ordered Etihad to be established (U.A.E.). In addition to Qatar, Oman, and Bahrain, Abu Dhabi has a stake in Gulf Air Co. However, Gulf Air's neighbors had decided to construct their national airlines, abandoning Gulf Air in favor of their own. These included Emirates Airlines of Dubai, which began service in 1985, and Qatar Airways Company Q.C.S.C., founded in 1992, which grew swiftly following their establishment.

Etihad's inception in 2003 was accompanied by rumors that Gulf Air would cohabit alongside Etihad, maybe as a feeder airline.

They all competed for business and tourism, but each had distinct advantages and disadvantages. There was a noticeable difference between the economies of Abu Dhabi and Dubai in terms of retail and tourism, for example. There was more to these places than just a stopover. When long-range aircraft were introduced, linking far-off locations became an essential component of the business strategy for Gulf-based airlines. These sites competed with previously dominating main hubs in Europe and Asia in terms of their capacity to link continents.

As a start-up airline in 2004, Etihad had a fleet of only six aircraft. This year, it made news when it placed an $8 billion purchase for new planes. All four Airbus A380 models under development were included among the 24 new aircraft. To accommodate the A380's size, this acquisition was risky and contentious. When other airlines were demanding compensation from Airbus for delays in its development, Etihad was negotiating with the company to delay certain deliveries.
